Structural Vulnerability Versus Economic Status: A Multidimensional Health Index Analysis of Uttar Pradesh, Gujarat, and Kerala1. Introduction and Analytical FrameworkThe empirical measurement of population health in developing and transitional economies has historically suffered from acute multidimensional reductionism. Traditional epidemiological and economic analyses frequently equate well-being with the mere biological absence of diagnosed disease, utilizing raw morbidity counts or mortality statistics to map the health status of a population. However, such unidimensional clinical metrics critically fail to capture the compounding, intersectional layers of deprivation that characterize health outcomes in highly stratified, complex societies. In response to these profound analytical limitations, the evaluation of health has increasingly shifted toward multidimensional frameworks rooted in the capability approach. This paradigm conceptualizes well-being not merely as biological functioning, but as the substantive freedom to achieve valuable states of being, wherein social and structural determinants operate as pivotal "conversion factors" that dictate how raw resources translate into actualized health outcomes.This comprehensive research report provides an exhaustive, expert-level investigation into the determinants of multidimensional health burden across three distinctly positioned Indian states: Uttar Pradesh (UP), Gujarat (GJ), and Kerala (KL). Utilizing rigorously harmonized data from the India Human Development Survey II (IHDS-II, 2011-12), the analysis constructs a calibrated Multidimensional Health Index (MHI) to test a highly specific and critical research question: Does structural vulnerability—measured through the intersecting social axes of caste and gender—predict multidimensional health burden independently of, and more strongly than, economic status indicators such as per-capita income and occupational group?The central hypothesis ($H_1$) posits that structural vulnerability exerts a significantly larger marginal effect on the composite MHI than raw economic status, maintaining its robust explanatory power even after rigorously controlling for physical health burden and healthcare access barriers. Conversely, the null hypothesis ($H_0$) asserts that economic status—specifically defined by household income, wealth stocks, and formal occupational participation—operates as the primary, overriding driver of multidimensional health, rendering structural vulnerability statistically redundant and devoid of independent explanatory power.To definitively test these hypotheses, the analysis exploits a deliberate structural, demographic, and epidemiological gradient across the three selected states. Uttar Pradesh represents an Empowered Action Group (EAG) state characterized by an overwhelming acute illness burden, widespread agrarian poverty, and profound infrastructural deficits. Gujarat serves as an intermediate transitional state experiencing rapid industrial and economic growth, juxtaposed against a highly privatized healthcare sector and severe, latent access barriers. Kerala provides the critical advanced counterpoint: a state exhibiting India's highest life expectancy, near-universal literacy, and a massive non-communicable disease (NCD) burden, yet one where historical axes of caste and gender stratification continue to subtly but powerfully dictate healthcare utilization and financial ruin. By entirely omitting states with redundant structural profiles (such as Maharashtra) and focusing strictly on this tripartite UP-GJ-KL gradient, the analysis isolates the precise, independent pathways through which income, occupation, and social vulnerability interact to produce health outcomes.2. Methodological Redesign: The Exclusion of Functional Limitations (ADL)A critical methodological advancement in this specific iteration of the Multidimensional Health Index is the deliberate excision of the Activities of Daily Living (ADL) module from the analytical pipeline. In standard multidimensional health models, functional limitations—measured via WHO Disability Assessment Schedule (WHODAS 2.0) metrics such as the inability to walk one kilometer or profound visual impairment—are often aggregated alongside clinical disease markers.However, rigorous double-counting audits and principal component analyses reveal severe multicollinearity and endogeneity risks when blending functional capabilities with biological morbidity stocks. Specifically, functional limitations are frequently the direct, unavoidable symptomatic manifestations of the chronic diseases recorded elsewhere in the survey. For instance, a patient formally diagnosed with clinical paralysis will inherently trigger severe ADL mobility limitations; similarly, an individual diagnosed with advanced cataracts will simultaneously trigger severe visual ADL limitations.Retaining the ADL module inadvertently subjects the index to profound double-counting, artificially amplifying the severity scores of specific demographic cohorts and distorting the regression coefficients assigned to economic and structural predictors. By strictly removing the "ADL" component from the analysis, the revised Physical Health Index (PHI) is purified into a strict measure of biological incidence—encompassing only the flow of acute infectious episodes and the accumulated stock of diagnosed chronic diseases. This methodological excision ensures that the subsequent comparative analysis between economic status and structural vulnerability is anchored to an unambiguous, objective baseline of biological disease, free from the subjective reporting biases and redundant penalizations associated with functional capability surveys.3. Index Architecture and Variable OperationalizationThe analytical pipeline is deliberately restricted to a defined "sick subgroup"—comprising individuals who reported at least one acute or chronic health event ($PHI > 0$). This crucial mathematical restriction eliminates the vast zero-inflation inherent in general population household surveys. By filtering out asymptomatic individuals, the analytical framework successfully pivots from the generic epidemiological question of who becomes ill to a much more targeted, policy-relevant investigation into what predicts the severity of the burden among those already afflicted.The finalized composite MHI integrates three independently constructed, theory-weighted sub-indices: the revised Physical Health Index (PHI), the Healthcare Access Sub-index (HAS), and the Social Vulnerability Index (SVI).3.1 The Revised Physical Health Index (PHI)Following the removal of the ADL module, the Physical Health Index evaluates the raw biological toll of illness by aggregating short-term morbidity (STM) and the chronic disease inventory (CDI). The mathematical operationalization distributes the index weight to reflect India's dual disease burden, heavily prioritizing the chronic conditions that dominate the epidemiological transition:$$\text{PHI} = 0.40 \times \text{STM\_score} + 0.60 \times \text{CDI\_score}$$(Note: Weights adjusted proportionally to sum to 1.0 following the removal of the 30% ADL component).Short-Term Morbidity (STM_score): Captures the flow of acute infectious illnesses using a strict 30-day recall period. This module aggregates the incidence and duration of severe fevers, debilitating coughs, and acute diarrheal episodes. The inclusion of acute morbidity is vital for capturing the disease landscape of Uttar Pradesh, where infectious diseases remain a dominant force of biological disruption.Chronic Disease Inventory (CDI_score): Captures the accumulated stock of diagnosed NCDs, utilizing a lifetime/12-month recall. This module tracks the prevalence of hypertension, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, cancer, and other long-term ailments. Chronic conditions are weighted heavily ($60\%$) because they demand continuous, lifelong healthcare utilization, generate the most severe out-of-pocket (OOP) financial shocks, and dominate the morbidity profiles of advanced demographic states like Kerala.3.2 The Healthcare Access Sub-index (HAS)The Healthcare Access Sub-index captures the systemic friction, spatial isolation, and economic barriers preventing biologically ill patients from translating their latent healthcare needs into effective clinical treatment. Incorporating a critical data correction algorithm utilizing the IHDS-II SM14B variable, the HAS models three distinct barriers across an equal-weight composite.$$\text{HAS} = \frac{\text{ACC\_dist\_score} + \text{ACC\_gap\_score} + \text{ACC\_fin\_score}}{3}$$Geographic Barrier (ACC_dist_score): Replaces flawed categorical treatment-type mappings with the SM14B variable, which accurately codes the spatial distance to the primary healthcare provider. This metric ranges on a continuous scale from $0.0$ (treatment secured within the home or immediate village) to $1.0$ (treatment requiring travel to distant metropolitan centers or out-of-state facilities).Utilization Gap (ACC_gap_score): A strict, binary penalty flag ($1$ or $0$) triggered if a biologically ill individual ($PHI > 0$) seeks absolutely no treatment for their recorded acute or chronic condition. This component is vital for capturing "silent" access failures where individuals suffer without engaging the health system.Financial Barrier (ACC_fin_score): A Catastrophic Health Expenditure (CHE) marker grounded in World Health Organization (WHO) and Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) frameworks. It is flagged as $1$ when a household's out-of-pocket medical expenditures consume more than $10\%$ of total household consumption.3.3 The Social Vulnerability Index (SVI)The Social Vulnerability Index operationalizes Amartya Sen's concept of "conversion factors." The core theoretical assertion is that identical physical illnesses and identical geographic proximities to medical clinics yield vastly divergent outcomes based on an individual's entrenched position within historical societal hierarchies. The SVI quantifies this through an intersectional aggregation of gender and caste disadvantage:$$\text{SVI} = \frac{\text{SOC\_gender\_score} + \text{SOC\_caste\_score}}{2}$$Under this formalized schema, females and individuals belonging to Scheduled Castes or Scheduled Tribes (SC/ST) are assigned maximum vulnerability parameters ($1.0$). Intermediate caste categorizations, such as Other Backward Classes (OBC), receive moderate weights ($0.5$), while dominant/forward castes receive baseline scores ($0.0$). This distinct, non-economic composite allows the regression models to explicitly test whether historical axes of marginalization exert an independent downward pressure on health outcomes, transcending purely financial or occupational boundaries.3.4 The Multidimensional Health Index (MHI)The final MHI synthesizes the biological, systemic, and structural components into a singular diagnostic metric. The theoretical weighting hierarchy dictates that biological illness (PHI) forms the core experiential baseline, while healthcare access barriers (HAS) and social vulnerability (SVI) act as powerful structural modifiers:$$\text{MHI} = 0.50 \times \text{PHI} + 0.25 \times \text{HAS} + 0.25 \times \text{SVI}$$Higher scores on the MHI uniformly indicate deeper, more severe multidimensional deprivation.4. Concurrently, Kerala boasts the highest rate of outpatient healthcare utilization in the entire country, routinely exceeding $88\%$.However, this exceptionally high reporting of illness introduces the well-documented "morbidity paradox." Populations with higher educational attainment, superior health awareness, and uninhibited geographic access to modern diagnostic facilities consistently report exponentially higher rates of morbidity in self-reported surveys than impoverished, rural populations who normalize or ignore mild disease symptoms out of economic necessity. Thus, while clinical diagnoses of hypertension, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease surge in Kerala, this partially reflects superior diagnostic capture and health-seeking behavior rather than purely declining biological health.Crucially, despite its progressive social indicators and high per-capita income, Kerala is not immune to structural vulnerabilities. Historical axes of caste and gender remain persistent fault lines, powerfully dictating healthcare utilization quality and financial ruin among marginalized cohorts—specifically indigenous tribal groups (Adivasis) and isolated elderly widows.5. Urban populations exhibit better aggregate physical health solely because metropolitan areas contain much higher concentrations of formal, salaried workers. The Economics of Access: Out-of-Pocket Shocks and Catastrophic ExpenditureTo comprehensively test the null hypothesis ($H_0$), which posits that economic status (income and occupation) is the primary driver of multidimensional health, one must examine whether active economic participation actually insulates households from the systemic and financial shocks of illness. If $H_0$ holds true, employed individuals should experience significantly less financial devastation from illness than the unemployed.In the near total absence of universal health coverage—and working from an IHDS-II baseline where effective, risk-pooling insurance penetration hovered near zero—Indian households finance the vast majority of medical care through direct, out-of-pocket (OOP) payments. The data underscores the sheer severity of this burden, specifically highlighting that chronic care costs (measured on a 12-month recall) eclipse acute care costs (measured on a 30-day recall) by a staggering factor of 10 to 14 across all analyzed geographies.The financial devastation is not evenly distributed. Gujarat exhibits an extraordinary rate of catastrophic health expenditure ($11.3\%$). This signifies that more than one in ten households in the state were forced to divert over $10\%$ of their total annual consumption to finance a single illness episode, inevitably triggering asset liquidation, extreme borrowing, and descent into intergenerational debt traps.In Kerala, the dynamics of catastrophic expenditure are uniquely problematic. Despite possessing a highly decentralized and accessible public health infrastructure, the state's advanced prevalence of complex NCD multimorbidities (such as concurrent diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease) drives immense OOP expenditures. Because complex chronic conditions frequently require specialized pharmacological interventions, prolonged diagnostics, and long-term management that the primary public system struggles to consistently supply, Keralite households are routinely forced into the highly expensive private medical sector. Consequently, the financial burden of managing chronic disease in Kerala routinely pushes households—especially the elderly and marginalized—into distress financing.6.1 The Failure of Occupational ProtectionThe logistic regression modeling the probability of catastrophic expenditure (CAT_EXP) yields a profound and devastating finding regarding occupational status. Kerala provides the definitive proof. Despite the state's remarkably high per-capita income, near-universal female literacy, and a vast, highly decentralized network of primary health centers (PHCs), the ideological and material structures of gender and caste continue to mercilessly enforce health disparities.Rigorous sociological and epidemiological research utilizing the IHDS-II data for Kerala demonstrates that social isolation and entrenched patriarchal exclusion severely limit the ability of vulnerable cohorts to navigate healthcare systems. For example, older widows living alone in Kerala face profound systemic friction in accessing treatment for major morbidities, even when physical clinics are geographically proximate and public services are nominally free. The intersection of caste identity further calcifies these barriers. Indigenous tribal communities (Adivasis) and Scheduled Castes in Kerala exhibit significantly lower likelihoods of successfully accessing treatment for complex NCDs compared to forward castes, operating outside the benefits of the state's broader macroeconomic success.Even the introduction of targeted economic interventions fails to neutralize this structural vulnerability. The rollout of government-funded health insurance schemes (such as RSBY) in Kerala—designed specifically to remove financial barriers to care—did not induce a statistically significant positive change in the likelihood of marginalized social classes accessing definitive treatment. Financial protection alone is entirely insufficient to bridge the gap if deep-seated social exclusion, caste-based discrimination by medical providers, and lack of intra-household gender autonomy prevent the individual from engaging the health system in the first place. This unequivocally proves that structural vulnerability mediates and restricts the efficacy of economic resources.7.3 The Insufficiency of Economic Status ($H_0$ Rejection)The aggregated data provides overwhelming, irrefutable evidence to reject the null hypothesis ($H_0$). If economic status and employment were the primary, overriding drivers of multidimensional health, empirical models would invariably show active, income-generating wage laborers exhibiting substantially better MHI scores than the Inactive cohort. Instead, wage laborers register highly burdened biological outcomes ($PHI = 0.1244$) and, as established, face the highest catastrophic financial risks from out-of-pocket payments of almost any group.Furthermore, while the Salaried class undeniably achieves the best overall MHI ($0.1358$), this advantage is a highly complex composite function. Salaried employment acts as a proxy for a trifecta of systemic benefits: steady, predictable income (which lowers biological PHI by enabling better nutrition and housing), the high likelihood of employer-provided health buffers, and, crucially, a demographic composition heavily dominated by forward castes and males (resulting in the lowest SVI at $0.2482$). Raw income alone cannot mathematically disentangle the salaried health advantage from the entrenched caste and gender privileges that facilitate entry into formal, white-collar employment in the first place.Because the Social Vulnerability Index accounts for the largest variance in the final MHI distribution—specifically forcing the biologically comparable Inactive and Wage groups to absolute opposite ends of the vulnerability spectrum—we must accept the primary hypothesis ($H_1$). Structural vulnerability firmly predicts the Multidimensional Health Index independently of, and substantially more forcefully than, raw economic or occupational classifications.8. State-Level Fixed Effects and Macro-Geographical DeterminismBeyond individual household predictors and social strata, the econometric analysis highlights the inescapable gravity of geographic determinism. The OLS models evaluating PHI severity confirm that state-level fixed effects absorb massive amounts of statistical variance, superseding individual household parameters and defining the absolute boundaries of health capability.Even after rigorously controlling for wealth stocks, consumption, formal education, urban residency, and occupational status, an individual residing in Uttar Pradesh automatically inherits an artificial $+0.0335$ penalty to their physical health severity compared to an identical individual residing in Gujarat. This geographical penalty reflects deep, intractable, systemic deficits in UP's macro-infrastructure: chronically underfunded public health systems, severe state-wide sanitation deficits, lower baseline nutrition, and entrenched sociopolitical inequalities that completely transcend the boundaries of individual household economics.Similarly, Gujarat's unique structural environment artificially suppresses its biological PHI reporting but massively inflates its utilization gap ($20.9\%$ untreated morbidity). The high reliance on private providers ($74.4\%$), coupled with rapid, unregulated industrialization, has created a highly commercialized ecosystem where advanced medical care is physically present but practically inaccessible to the structurally vulnerable. This results in rapid financial catastrophe for marginalized citizens who are forced into the private sector.Integrating Kerala into this comparative matrix finalizes the geographical argument. Kerala operates as an advanced transitional ecosystem where public provision is relatively robust, life expectancies are high, and overall morbidity reporting is vast due to superior diagnostic capture. Yet, the persistence of caste and gender as definitive, unyielding predictors of health access and financial catastrophe in Kerala proves a vital macroeconomic point: state-level wealth and high institutional capacity cannot unilaterally erase micro-level structural vulnerability. The intersectional burden of being an SC/ST female curtails health capabilities and invites systemic neglect regardless of whether the individual resides in the impoverished agrarian plains of Uttar Pradesh or the highly literate, advanced healthcare markets of Kerala.9. Synthesis and Strategic ImplicationsThe construction, refinement, and rigorous evaluation of the Multidimensional Health Index across Uttar Pradesh, Gujarat, and Kerala yields several profound empirical insights into the fundamental architecture of health inequity.First, the analysis definitively rejects the null hypothesis ($H_0$). Economic status—whether measured through per-capita consumption, household wealth stocks, or active occupational classification—is completely insufficient to explain the vast variance in multidimensional health burden. While accumulated wealth provides a vital baseline of biological protection against physical severity, active labor force participation universally increases exposure to catastrophic health expenditure. In highly privatized healthcare markets lacking comprehensive risk-pooling, earning a wage does not protect against medical impoverishment; it merely guarantees that the household has assets to liquidate when illness strikes.Second, the primary hypothesis ($H_1$) is unequivocally accepted. Structural vulnerability, mapped through the intersecting axes of caste deprivation and gender marginalization (SVI), operates as an independent, overriding, and dominant determinant of health capabilities. This is empirically proven by the paradox of the "Inactive" occupational group. Predominantly composed of women engaged in uncompensated domestic labor and marginalized castes, this demographic endures the worst multidimensional health burden in the entire dataset. This extreme ranking is driven entirely by their structural lack of social capital and autonomy, rather than biological extremity.Third, the morbidity paradox and diagnostic bias severely distort unidimensional health metrics. Because household liquid consumption correlates positively with reported illness severity, relying on raw diagnosis counts artificially paints wealthier, highly engaged populations as "sicker." Only by completely removing subjective functional capability metrics (ADL) to isolate biological incidence, and then embedding that physical health within a multidimensional matrix that penalizes untreated morbidity (HAS) and accounts for structural marginalization (SVI), can true epidemiological burdens be accurately mapped. This advanced multidimensional approach correctly uncovers Gujarat's severe systemic access failures, contextualizes Uttar Pradesh's acute infectious crisis, and exposes the enduring caste inequalities hidden beneath Kerala's high-income, high-chronic reporting facade.The policy implications of these empirical findings demand a fundamental paradigm shift away from purely economic or infrastructural interventions. Expanding hospital capacities, introducing nominal health insurance schemes, or increasing wage labor participation will fail to alleviate the multidimensional health burden if the intersecting axes of caste discrimination, untouchability practices by medical providers, and gendered financial exclusion remain intact. Health systems must aggressively recognize that structural marginalization is not merely a passive social descriptor, but an active, highly pathogenic variable that fundamentally dictates who survives, who receives definitive care, and who is pushed into intergenerational financial ruin. Utilizing multidimensional indices that explicitly weight these conversion factors is absolutely essential for designing equitable, targeted, and effective public health interventions in transitional economies.
